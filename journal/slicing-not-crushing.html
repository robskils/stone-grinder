Slicing, not crushing: a study of the cut

19 June 2026 5 min read

Pick up a generic grinder, twist it, and listen. You will hear a crunch. Those wide, blunt pegs are not cutting anything - they are pressing, tearing, and bruising the herb between two flat plates until it gives way. It works, in the way that standing on a tomato works. The result is a mush of flattened, oxidising material with its most fragile and valuable parts smeared across the metal.

A knife does not work like that. A good blade parts what it meets, cleanly, with as little crushing as physics allows. That was the brief for the inside of the STONE: stop pressing, start cutting.

Thirty teeth, three rings, one job

Inside the STONE sit thirty elongated-diamond teeth, arranged in three concentric rings. The geometry is deliberate. Each tooth is tall - 6.5mm - and shaped to a true edge rather than a stub, so it meets the material side-on and slices through it. The three rings are offset so that as the halves turn, material is handed inward from ring to ring, getting a clean, progressive cut rather than a single brutal mash.

The diamond profile matters more than it sounds. A square peg presents a flat face and shoves. A diamond presents a leading edge, then relieves behind it, so the cut material falls away instead of packing into the gaps. Less packing means less clogging, less wasted effort, and a consistency that looks chopped rather than pulverised.

The difference between crushing and slicing is the difference between what you ground and what you wasted.

Why the bruise costs you

The most prized parts of cannabis flower are also the most delicate: the trichomes, those frosted resin heads where the aroma and potency actually live. Crush them against a flat plate and they rupture, oxidise, and stick to the metal - the dull film you scrape off a cheap grinder is, quite literally, the best of what you paid for, left behind.

A clean slice keeps those heads intact for longer. The cut material holds its structure, its colour, and its terpene profile - the bright, particular smell that crushing flattens into something generic. You notice it first in the aroma when you open the lid, and again in everything downstream of it.

The glide is part of the cut

A sharp edge is only half of a clean cut. The other half is control. Most grinders wobble on a loose central post, so the teeth meet at inconsistent angles and the action feels gritty and vague. The STONE turns instead on a fully sealed internal bearing track, machined to a 21mm radius.

The effect is hard to overstate until your hand feels it. There is no play, no grind, no catch. The two halves move with the slow, weighted certainty of a vault door swinging shut - friction-free, perfectly concentric, every tooth meeting the material at exactly the angle it was designed to. And because the four-lobe S-Curve gives your fingers real leverage, even dense, sticky flower yields without a fight. For anyone with weak grip or tired hands, that leverage is not a luxury. It is the difference between a tool you can use and one you cannot.

Engineering you taste

None of this is visible while it happens. It is sealed inside cold steel, turning quietly in your palm. But you meet the result every time you open the lid: a clean, even, fragrant cut that held on to everything worth holding on to. The teeth do the work. The bearing keeps them honest. You just turn.

Feel the glide.
