Why 316L is the only steel that belongs in your hand

12 June 2026 4 min read

There is a number stamped, quietly, into the underside of almost every object you actually trust. The clasp of a good watch. The blade of a kitchen knife worth keeping. The instruments a surgeon lays out before they begin. That number is 316L, and once you start looking for it, you notice that it tends to appear wherever failure is not an option.

The grinder in your drawer almost certainly does not carry it. Most are cast from cheap zinc alloy or anodised aluminium - materials chosen because they are soft, fast to machine, and cost almost nothing. They do the job for a season. Then the anodising wears at the teeth, the threads pick up grit, and a faint metallic tang starts to creep into everything that passes through. You replace it, and the cycle begins again.

We did not want to make a grinder you replace. So we started with the material, and worked backwards.

What the L actually means

316 is an austenitic stainless steel alloyed with chromium, nickel, and - crucially - molybdenum. That molybdenum is what separates it from the 304 steel in your cutlery drawer. It is the reason 316 is the grade specified for marine fittings, chemical tanks, and the housings of dive watches rated to depths no person will ever visit. It simply refuses to corrode.

The L stands for low carbon. Less carbon means the steel resists the microscopic breakdown that happens at grain boundaries, which is exactly why it is the grade chosen for surgical implants - the pins and plates that live inside the human body for decades without reacting to it. It is, by any honest definition, food-safe and body-safe. Nothing leaches. Nothing tarnishes. Nothing transfers.

An object you bring to your mouth, daily, for years, deserves the same material as a thing left inside a body for a lifetime.

That is not marketing. It is the whole argument. An object you handle every day, that touches what you put into your body, should be held to the standard of the most demanding applications we have - not the cheapest.

Why it had to be forged this way

316L is notoriously difficult to shape. It is hard, it work-hardens as you cut it, and it punishes shortcuts. The easy path is to stamp thin sheet and call it stainless. We went the other way, using metal injection molding to form the full geometry as solid, dense steel - the sweeping lobes, the elongated-diamond teeth, the sealed bearing track - all in one honest piece.

The result has a specific consequence you feel before you understand it: weight. Real 316L is dense. When you lift the STONE, your hand expects the light, hollow tap of aluminium and instead meets the cold, settled mass of something serious. That moment of recalibration, that small surprise in the palm, is the material telling the truth about itself.

The last one you buy

Cheap grinders are a subscription you did not realise you signed up for. A few euros at a time, a new one every year, each slightly worse than the last. 316L breaks that loop. It does not pit, does not stain, does not dull, and does not care how many years pass. Run it under hot water, dry it, and it looks exactly as it did the day it arrived.

That permanence is the point. Choose the material that outlasts the habit, and you only ever have to choose once.
